Measures of the Center

Mean

  • Arithmetic Mean: \(\mu = \Sigma x_i / N\), \(\bar x = \Sigma x_i / n\).

Demo:

The daily temperatures for last week were \(t_1 = 27, t_2 = 26, t_3 = 30, t_4 = 27, t_5 = 29, t_6 = 30, t_7 = 25 ^\circ\)C. The mean temperature of last week was \(\bar t = \Sigma t_i / n\) \(^\circ\)C.

(27+ 26 + 30 + 27 + 29 + 30 + 25) / 7
## [1] 27.71429
tc <- c(27, 26, 30, 27, 29, 30, 25)
sum(tc)/length(tc)
## [1] 27.71429
mean(tc)
## [1] 27.71429
  • If \(y_i = x_i + c\), then \(\bar y = \bar x + c\). This is called a translated sample.

Demo:

Calculate the mean daily temperature of last week in Kelvin, \([K] = [^\circ \text C] + 273\).

## [1] 300.7143
mean(tc) + 273
## [1] 300.7143

## [1] TRUE
  • If \(y_i = cx_i\), then \(\bar y = c \bar x\). This is called a scaled sample.

Demo:

  • If \(y_i = c_1 x_i + c_2\), then \(\bar y = c_1 \bar x + c_2\). This is called a transformed sample.

Demo:

Calculate the mean daily temperature of last week in degree Fahrenheit, \([^\circ \text F] = [^\circ \text C] \times 9/5 + 32\).

## [1] 81.88571
  • Geometric Mean: \(G=\sqrt[n] {\Pi x_i}\)

Demo:

An orange tree yielded 100 oranges in 2016, then 180, 210 and 300 in 2017, 2018, and 2019, respectively, which means the yield increased by 80%, 17%, and 43%, respectively. What is the mean annual growth rate?

## [1] 301.158

The arithmetic mean of the growth rate makes no sense in this case.

## [1] 315.4963
  • Harmonic Mean: \(H = n/\Sigma \frac{1}{x_i}\)

Demo:

The harmonic mean is meaningful. Suppose the distance between Wenxing Square and ES209 is 1.1 km, then we can calculate the time Tom spent:

Median

Median:
Middle value of an ordered set. Also called \(Q_2\) and \(P_{50}\).
  • If n is odd, the median is the \((n+1)/2\) -th largest observation
  • If n is even, the median is the mean of the \(n/2\) -th and the \((n+1)/2\) -th largest observation

Measures Paired with the Arithmetic Mean

  • Variance: for a population, \(\sigma^2 = \frac{\Sigma (x_i - \mu)^2}{N}\); for a sample, \(s^2 = \frac{\Sigma (x_i - \bar x)^2}{n-1}\)

  • Standard deviation: \(\sigma\), \(s\)

  • Coefficient of variation: \(c_v = \sigma / \mu\), \(c_v = s / \bar x\)

Measures Paired with the Median

  • Percentiles (or quartiles)

    • The p-th percentile is the value \(V_p\) such that p% of the sample points are less than or equal to \(V_p\).

    • Three quartiles: \(Q_1 = P_{25}\), \(Q_2 = P_{50} = \mathrm {median}\), \(Q_3 = P_{75}\).

    • Interquartile range: \(\text{IQR} = Q_3 â Q_1 = P_{75} â P_{25}\)

If the 25th percentile (or the first quartile) of a dataset is 667 grams, then 25% of all data points are less than or equal to 667 grams.

Question: The y-scales are different in these four sub-figures, why?

Convert the frequency to density:

Density = Frequency / bin-width / range

For example, the frequency between 165 and 170 is 15 (15 persons are between 165 cm and 170 cm), and the range is \(186 - 150 = 36\). With the bin width of 5 cm, the density is \(15 / 5 / 46 = 0.065\).

Violin plot

A violin Plot is a combination of a box plot and a density plot that is rotated and placed on each side, to show the distribution shape of the data. The white dot in the middle is the median value and the thick black bar in the centre represents the interquartile range.

library(vioplot)
vioplot(h, horizontal = TRUE)

If you have problems with installing the viplot package, you could plot a violin plot with ggplot2:

library(ggplot2)
env203$All <- "All"
ggplot(env203, aes(x = All, y = Height)) +
  geom_violin() +
  geom_boxplot(width = 0.1) +
  coord_flip()
